## Overview

Saint Simon the Zealot, also known as Simon the Cananean, was one of the Twelve Apostles chosen by Jesus Christ. To distinguish him from Simon Peter, he is referred to as "the Zealot," reflecting his deep religious zeal or possible connection to the Zealot movement. 

According to ancient tradition, Simon preached the Gospel in Egypt, North Africa, and Persia alongside Saint Jude Thaddeus. He suffered a brutal martyrdom and is venerated as the patron saint of tanners, woodcutters, and sawyers.

## Identity and Discipleship

Simon appears in all New Testament lists of the Twelve Apostles, though the Gospels do not record the specific circumstances of his calling. The Gospels of Matthew and Mark call him "Simon the Cananean" (derived from the Hebrew word for "zealot"), while Luke refers to him as "Simon the Zealot." 

This title indicates either his intense passion for the Jewish law or his historical association with the Zealots, a political group devoted to defending Jewish independence. Following his call, he redirected his zeal to the service of Christ's kingdom.

## Mission with Saint Jude

Following Pentecost, Simon preached the Gospel throughout Egypt, Cyrene, and North Africa. Ancient Christian traditions state that he later joined Saint Jude Thaddeus in Mesopotamia. 

Together, they traveled into Persia (modern-day Iran), where they spent years evangelizing the population, converting thousands, and establishing early Christian communities. Because of their close missionary partnership, the Western Church has historically celebrated their feast day together.

## Martyrdom

Simon's missionary work in Persia eventually met with fierce resistance from local pagan priests. While preaching alongside Saint Jude, both Apostles were arrested and ordered to sacrifice to pagan idols. 

Upon refusing, Simon was martyred. While eastern traditions suggest he was crucified, the most common tradition in the West is that Simon was sawn in half with a large wooden saw. He bore this horrific torture with steadfast faith.

## Legacy and Relics

Because of the tradition of his death, Saint Simon's primary symbol in art is a large saw. He is also depicted carrying a book or scroll. 

His relics, alongside those of Saint Jude Thaddeus, were brought to Rome and rest beneath the altar of Saint Joseph in St. Peter's Basilica. He remains an inspiring example of redirecting worldly passion into holy zeal for the Gospel.
