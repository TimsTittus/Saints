## Overview

Saint Catherine of Siena — *Caterina Benincasa* — stands among the most formidable and influential women in Church history. A Dominican tertiary who never received formal theological training, she dictated letters and works of mystical theology that rank among the masterpieces of Italian spiritual literature. She convinced Pope Gregory XI to end the Avignon papacy and return the Bishop of Rome to Rome — a decisive act in one of the Church's greatest crises. In 1970, Pope Paul VI declared her a Doctor of the Church, and in 1999 Pope John Paul II named her a Patron of Europe.

## Early Life and Visions

Catherine was born on March 25, 1347, in Siena, the 24th of 25 children of Giacomo Benincasa, a cloth dyer, and Lapa Piagenti. From childhood she experienced religious visions. At age seven, she reportedly saw a vision of Christ seated in glory above the Basilica of San Domenico, which decided her religious destiny.

Her family hoped to marry her off, and her mother urged her to make herself more attractive. Catherine instead cut off her hair to make herself unappealing and fought a prolonged battle for her vocation.

## The Dominican Third Order

Catherine entered the Order of Preachers as a *mantellata* (a Dominican tertiary — a layperson living under a modified religious rule) at around age eighteen. For three years she lived almost entirely in her room, eating almost nothing, speaking only in confession, and experiencing intense mystical states.

Around 1368, she underwent what she called a "mystical marriage" with Christ — a locution in which Christ placed a ring on her finger and betrothed her to himself. After this she emerged from her seclusion to serve the sick and poor of Siena, including victims of the plague.

## Ministry and Mystical Life

Catherine's ministry was characterized by bold practical charity and deep contemplative prayer. She cared for victims of the Black Death and leprosy, reconciled enemies, and worked tirelessly for social peace in Siena's violent factional politics.

She is reported to have received the stigmata in 1375 — visible only to herself during her life, at her request, so as not to attract attention. Her spiritual depth attracted a large circle of followers — disciples who called themselves the *Caterinati* — including priests, laypeople, and even knights and nobles.

## Correspondence with Popes

Catherine's most astonishing interventions were political. In a series of bold letters to Pope Gregory XI (then residing at Avignon in France, where the papacy had relocated in 1309), she urged him with remarkable directness — calling him "Babbo mio" (*my little father*) — to return to Rome and reform the Church. Her letters were written with an urgency and authority that astonished readers then and now.

Gregory XI returned to Rome on January 17, 1377, ending the Avignon papacy. Catherine's role in this decision is historically acknowledged, though Gregory had multiple reasons for the move.

## The Great Schism

Catherine's final years were overshadowed by the Great Western Schism, which began in 1378 with the election of an antipope, Clement VII, supported by the French cardinals. Catherine wrote hundreds of letters defending Pope Urban VI's legitimacy and worked until her health gave out trying to heal the schism.

She suffered a stroke in January 1380 and died on April 29, 1380, at age 33. Her last words were reported as: *"Father, into your hands I commend my soul and my spirit."*

## The Dialogue

Catherine's major spiritual work, *The Dialogue of Divine Providence* (*Il Dialogo della Divina Provvidenza*), dictated in mystical states between 1377 and 1378, is structured as a conversation between a soul and God. It addresses the nature of divine providence, virtue, the ordained priesthood, obedience, and the "bridge" of Christ as the way to God. It is a foundational text of Christian mysticism.
