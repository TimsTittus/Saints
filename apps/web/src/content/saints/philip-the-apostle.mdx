## Overview

Saint Philip the Apostle was one of the Twelve chosen by Jesus Christ, called with the direct invitation, *"Follow me."* He immediately responded and famously introduced Nathanael (Saint Bartholomew) to Jesus, telling him to *"Come and see."* 

Philip witnessed many of Christ's miracles, including the Feeding of the Five Thousand. Following Pentecost, he preached the Gospel throughout Asia Minor and was martyred for his faith in Hierapolis around AD 80.

## Call to Discipleship

Philip was born in Bethsaida, Galilee—the same town as Saint Peter and Saint Andrew. He was personally called by Jesus on the day after Peter joined. Jesus simply approached him and said: *"Follow me."* Philip immediately obeyed. 

Shortly after, he found his friend Nathanael and shared the Good News: *"We have found Him about whom Moses in the law and also the prophets wrote, Jesus of Nazareth."* When Nathanael expressed skepticism, Philip provided the classic invitation to faith: *"Come and see."*

## Role in the Gospels

Philip plays a prominent role in several Gospel accounts, particularly in the Gospel of John. Before the miracle of the Feeding of the Five Thousand, Jesus tested Philip's faith by asking him: *"Where can we buy enough food for them to eat?"* Philip calculated the cost, noting that even two hundred denarii would not buy enough bread for each person to have a little, setting the stage for the miracle. 

Later, during the Last Supper, Philip made the request: *"Lord, show us the Father, and that will be enough for us."* Jesus answered with a core theological revelation: *"Whoever has seen me has seen the Father."*

## Mission and Martyrdom

Following the events of Pentecost, Philip set out to preach the Gospel. Ancient traditions record that he preached extensively in Greece, Syria, and Asia Minor. He performed many miracles, converting large crowds to Christianity. 

His mission ended in Hierapolis, Phrygia (modern Turkey), where his preaching angered local pagan priests. Philip was arrested, imprisoned, and subsequently crucified—traditionally upside down—witnessing to Christ until his final breath.

## Relics and Legacy

Saint Philip's relics were eventually translated to Rome, where they lie alongside those of Saint James the Less in the Basilica of the Holy Apostles (Santi Apostoli). 

In Christian art, Philip is traditionally depicted carrying a basket of loaves (representing the Feeding of the Five Thousand) or holding a large Latin cross (the instrument of his martyrdom). He is venerated as the patron saint of Uruguay, Cape Verde, hat makers, and pastry chefs.
