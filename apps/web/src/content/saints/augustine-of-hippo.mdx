## Overview

Saint Augustine of Hippo is arguably the most influential Christian theologian after the Apostle Paul. His *Confessions* — the first great autobiography in Western literature — and his monumental *City of God* reshaped how Christians understood themselves, their faith, and their relation to secular society. His writings on grace, predestination, original sin, and the nature of the Church sparked debates that echo to this day, from the Reformation to modern Catholic and Protestant theology alike.

## Early Life and Education

Augustine was born on November 13, 354, in Thagaste, a small town in the Roman province of Numidia (modern Algeria). His mother Monica — later canonized as <SaintLink slug="saint-monica">Saint Monica</SaintLink> — was a devout Christian who prayed fervently for her son's conversion. His father Patricius was a Roman pagan who converted to Christianity shortly before his death.

Augustine was an exceptionally gifted student. Educated at Madauros and Carthage, he became fascinated with rhetoric and philosophy. He read Cicero's *Hortensius* as a teenager and described how it ignited in him a passionate love of wisdom.

## The Restless Heart

Augustine's early adult life was marked by intellectual searching, moral struggle, and a nine-year adherence to Manichaeism — a dualistic religion that satisfied his desire for rational explanation. He also lived with a concubine for thirteen years, with whom he had a son, Adeodatus.

His Manichaean phase ended when he found Mani's philosophy intellectually unsatisfying. Traveling to Rome and then Milan, he encountered the sermons of Bishop Ambrose and the texts of Neoplatonist philosophers. He began to find intellectual pathways into Christian faith.

## The Garden at Milan

In the summer of 386, Augustine experienced his famous conversion in a Milan garden. Weeping under a fig tree, he heard a child's voice chanting *"Tolle, lege"* ("Take up and read"). Opening Paul's Letter to the Romans at random, he read words urging moral renewal — and felt his resistance dissolve. He described this moment in the *Confessions*: *"Our heart is restless, O Lord, until it rests in thee."*

## Baptism and Return to Africa

Augustine was baptized by Bishop Ambrose at the Easter Vigil in 387, alongside his son Adeodatus. His mother Monica died shortly after, at Ostia, while they awaited passage back to Africa — an event recorded with heartbreaking tenderness in the *Confessions*.

Returning to Africa, Augustine established a monastic community in Thagaste. In 391 he was ordained a priest at Hippo Regius against his will — the people effectively conscripted him — and in 395 he became the city's bishop, a role he held until his death.

## Major Works

- **Confessions** (397–400) — Spiritual autobiography and sustained prayer, tracing his journey to faith
- **City of God** (413–426) — A monumental defense of Christianity written in response to the sack of Rome; the foundational work of Christian political theology
- **On the Trinity** — A systematic exploration of Trinitarian doctrine
- **On Free Will**, **On Grace and Free Will** — Defining works on predestination and human freedom

## The Pelagian Controversy

Augustine's most fierce theological battle was against Pelagianism — the teaching of the British monk Pelagius that humans can achieve salvation through their own moral effort. Augustine countered with a profound doctrine of grace: human nature is damaged by original sin, and salvation depends entirely on God's freely given, undeserved grace. This controversy shaped his mature theology and has driven Catholic-Protestant debates ever since.

## Death During the Vandal Siege

Augustine died on August 28, 430, as the Vandal armies besieged Hippo. He spent his final days in prayer, reportedly ordering the penitential psalms written on sheets placed on his bedroom wall so he could gaze at them on his deathbed. He died nine years before Hippo itself fell.
