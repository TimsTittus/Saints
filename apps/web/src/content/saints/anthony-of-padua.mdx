## Overview

Saint Anthony of Padua — born in Lisbon but forever associated with the Italian city of Padua where he died — was one of the most gifted preachers of the medieval Church and one of the fastest canonized saints in history: he was canonized less than a year after his death. A contemporary of <SaintLink slug="francis-of-assisi">Saint Francis of Assisi</SaintLink>, Anthony exemplified Franciscan ideals of poverty, learning, and apostolic preaching in a remarkable but short life. He is beloved worldwide as the patron of lost things and the poor.

## Early Life in Lisbon

Anthony was born Fernando Martins de Bulhões on August 15, 1195, in Lisbon, Portugal, to a noble family. He was educated at the cathedral school and at fifteen entered the Augustinian Canons Regular, joining their house in Coimbra around 1212. There he immersed himself in the study of Scripture and theology under the influence of the canons' excellent library.

## Encounter with the Franciscan Martyrs

A decisive turning point came in 1220, when the relics of five Franciscan missionaries — martyred in Morocco — were brought to Coimbra for veneration. Deeply moved by their witness and seized by a desire for martyrdom himself, Anthony sought permission to transfer to the recently founded Franciscan Order, receiving it and taking the name Anthony.

## Illness and Hidden Gift

Anthony sailed for Morocco to pursue his missionary calling, but fell gravely ill and was forced to return. His ship was blown off course and landed in Sicily, from where he made his way to Assisi for a general chapter of the Franciscan Order in 1221 — probably meeting Francis himself. At the chapter, no one had a preaching assignment for Anthony, who appeared simply as an unknown Portuguese friar. He was assigned to serve at a hermitage near Forlì.

At an ordination ceremony where no preacher was prepared, the guardian of the hermitage asked Anthony to speak impromptu. What emerged astonished the assembled friars and clerics: a torrent of scriptural exposition, theological depth, and moving eloquence. Word reached Francis, who authorized Anthony's ministry of preaching.

## The Preacher of Europe

From 1222 onward, Anthony became the most famous preacher of his era. He preached across northern and central Italy and in southern France, attracting enormous crowds — reportedly including heretics who converted en masse. He was assigned by Francis himself to teach theology to the friars, a mandate Francis gave in a famous letter acknowledging Anthony's learning.

His preaching style combined rigorous Scripture scholarship with vivid illustrations drawn from nature. He is often called *the Hammer of Heretics* (*Malleus Hereticorum*) for his effectiveness against the Cathar heresy in southern France.

## The Miracle of the Mule

Among the many miracles attributed to Anthony, one concerns a Cathar nobleman who challenged him to prove the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist. The man would not believe unless his starved mule, offered hay, instead knelt before the Blessed Sacrament. Anthony accepted — and the mule did precisely that. This event has been commemorated in art across the centuries.

## The Sermon to the Fishes

When inhabitants of Rimini refused to hear Anthony's preaching, he reportedly went to the water's edge and preached to the fish, who gathered at the surface with their heads above water and listened attentively. The astonished townsfolk then came to hear him.

## Final Days and Death

By 1231, Anthony's health had collapsed under the strain of his apostolic labors. He went to rest at a woodland retreat at Camposampiero, north of Padua. There, he reportedly experienced a vision of the Christ Child. On June 13, 1231, he died at the Poor Clares' convent in Arcella, aged only 35.

The children of Padua reportedly ran through the streets crying: *"The Saint is dead! The Saint is dead!"*

## Canonization and Doctor of the Church

Pope Gregory IX canonized Anthony on May 30, 1232 — less than a year after his death — at Spoleto. In 1946, Pope Pius XII proclaimed him a Doctor of the Church, with the title *Doctor Evangelicus* (Evangelical Doctor).

## Patron of Lost Things

The tradition of praying to Anthony for help finding lost objects derives from an incident in which a novice who had taken Anthony's psalter (a book of psalms) returned it after Anthony prayed — reportedly prompted by a frightening apparition.
