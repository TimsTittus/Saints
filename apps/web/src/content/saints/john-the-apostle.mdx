## Overview

Saint John the Apostle, also known as Saint John the Evangelist or the Beloved Disciple, was one of the Twelve Apostles of Jesus Christ and the brother of Saint James the Greater. Known for his deep theological insights and emphasis on charity, he is often called the "Apostle of Love." 

John was a member of Jesus' inner circle, present at the Transfiguration and the Crucifixion, where Jesus entrusted the care of His mother, Mary, to him. He is traditionally credited with writing the Gospel of John, three epistles, and the Book of Revelation, and is believed to have died of natural causes in Ephesus around AD 100.

## Early Life and Calling

Born in Galilee in the early 1st century AD, John was the son of Zebedee and Salome. He worked as a fisherman alongside his brother James on the Sea of Galilee. 

While mending nets with their father, Jesus approached the brothers and called them. They immediately left their nets, their boat, and their father to follow Him, showcasing a remarkable spirit of immediate obedience and faith that characterized their discipleship.

## Son of Thunder and Beloved Disciple

Jesus gave James and John the nickname *Boanerges*, meaning "Sons of Thunder," likely due to their passionate, zealous, and sometimes fiery temperaments. Despite this intensity, John developed a uniquely close relationship with Jesus, traditionally identifying himself in his Gospel as "the disciple whom Jesus loved." 

Along with Peter and James, John belonged to Jesus' inner circle, witnessing key events such as the raising of Jairus' daughter, the Transfiguration, and the Agony in Gethsemane.

## At the Foot of the Cross

When Jesus was arrested and crucified, most of the disciples fled in fear. John, however, displayed heroic courage by standing faithfully at the foot of the Cross alongside the Blessed Virgin Mary and other holy women. 

Seeing his mother and the beloved disciple, Jesus spoke from the Cross, saying to Mary, *"Woman, behold your son,"* and to John, *"Behold your mother."* From that hour, John took Mary into his home and cared for her until the end of her earthly life.

## Exile to Patmos and Writings

Following Pentecost, John preached throughout Judea and later settled in Ephesus, leading the Church in Asia Minor. During the reign of Emperor Domitian, he faced persecution and was exiled to the rocky island of Patmos. 

There, he received the prophetic and mystical visions recorded in the Book of Revelation. Christian tradition credits John with writing five books of the New Testament: the Gospel of John, three Epistles of John, and the Book of Revelation, all containing profound themes of love, light, truth, and communion with God.

## Death and Legacy

Unlike the other Apostles who all suffered violent martyrdoms, John is traditionally believed to have survived persecutions (including being thrown into boiling oil, from which he emerged unharmed) and died peacefully of natural causes in Ephesus around AD 100. 

In Christian art, he is symbolized by the eagle, representing the lofty theological height of his Gospel, which begins with the famous prologue: *"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."*
