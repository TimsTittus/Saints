## Overview

Saint Mary Magdalene holds a singular place in the Gospels: she was a devoted follower of Jesus, a witness to the Crucifixion, and the first person to whom the Risen Christ appeared on Easter morning — earning her the title *Apostola Apostolorum*, "Apostle to the Apostles." In 2016, Pope Francis elevated her feast day to the rank of Feast, equal to that of the Apostles, a recognition of her foundational role in Christian faith.

## Identity and Biblical Record

Mary Magdalene is named in all four Gospel accounts, identified as coming from Magdala, a prosperous fishing town on the western shore of the Sea of Galilee. The Gospel of Luke (8:2) identifies her as one from whom "seven demons had gone out" — most scholars interpret this as a severe illness, physical or mental, from which Jesus healed her, rather than a reference to moral failing.

She is distinct from the unnamed sinful woman who anointed Jesus's feet (Luke 7:37–50), though Western tradition — particularly following a homily by Pope Gregory I in 591 — conflated these figures. The Eastern Church always maintained her distinct identity. In 1969, the Roman Catholic Church formally separated her from the other figures.

## Witness to the Passion

Mary Magdalene was present at the Crucifixion when most of the male disciples had fled. All four Gospels name her among the women who watched from near the cross. She was present at the tomb when Jesus's body was laid, and she noted carefully where the tomb was located.

## The First Witness to the Resurrection

Early on the morning of the first day of the week, Mary Magdalene came to the tomb while it was still dark (John 20:1). Finding it empty, she ran to tell Peter and John. Returning to weep outside the tomb, she encountered a figure she took to be a gardener — until he spoke her name: *"Mary."* She turned and cried: *"Rabboni!"* (Teacher).

Christ's charge to her — *"Go to my brothers and tell them"* (John 20:17) — makes her the first missionary of the Resurrection, the Apostle to the Apostles.

## Post-Resurrection Traditions

After the Ascension, traditions diverge. Eastern Orthodox tradition holds that Mary Magdalene traveled to Ephesus with the Virgin Mary and Saint John and died there. Western tradition, particularly strong in Provence, France, holds that she sailed to the south of France and spent her final years as a hermit in the cave of La Sainte-Baume, evangelizing the region.

## Legacy and Liturgical Reform

Mary Magdalene's feast day of July 22 has been observed since at least the 8th century. Pope Francis's 2016 decision to elevate it to a Feast — the same liturgical rank as the Apostles — was accompanied by a decree noting her role as "the first to announce the joy of the resurrection."

She has been the subject of enormous artistic, literary, and scholarly attention. Countless Renaissance masterworks depict her, and she remains one of the most recognizable figures from the New Testament.
