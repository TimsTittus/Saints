## Overview

Saint Francis of Assisi, born Giovanni di Pietro di Bernardone around 1181–1182, is one of the most venerated religious figures in history. The founder of the Franciscan Order, he is celebrated for his radical embrace of poverty, his profound love of nature, and his mystical reception of the stigmata — becoming the first recorded person to bear the wounds of Christ's crucifixion. His life and teachings reshaped Christian spirituality in the medieval world and continue to inspire millions.

## Early Life and Youth

Francis was born to Pietro di Bernardone, a prosperous cloth merchant, and his wife Lady Pica, likely of French origin, in the city of Assisi in Umbria, central Italy. His father, returning from a business trip to France, renamed the boy Francesco ("the Frenchman") in honor of his affection for French culture. The young Francis grew up enjoying the privileges of a wealthy merchant's son — good food, fine clothes, and revelries with friends. He harbored dreams of becoming a knight.

In 1202, Francis participated in a conflict between Assisi and Perugia and was captured, spending about a year as a prisoner of war. He returned home ill and began a prolonged spiritual crisis that would mark the beginning of his transformation.

## Conversion and Call

In 1205, Francis set out to join a military expedition but turned back after a visionary dream. He spent increasing time alone in prayer, visiting the poor, and serving lepers, a group he had previously found repulsive. A decisive moment came while he was praying in the ruined chapel of San Damiano, where he heard the voice of Christ say: *"Francis, repair my Church, which is falling into ruins."*

Taking these words literally at first, he sold some of his father's cloth to fund repairs to the chapel. This led to a dramatic public confrontation in which his father brought him before the Bishop of Assisi. Francis renounced his inheritance, stripped off his fine clothes, and declared God alone as his father — a radical act of spiritual poverty.

## Founding of the Franciscan Order

Living as a wandering penitent, Francis attracted followers drawn to his joyful simplicity. In 1209, he composed a short rule of life based on Gospel passages emphasizing poverty, humility, and mission. He and his eleven companions walked to Rome to seek approval from Pope Innocent III, who granted verbal authorization for their community — the beginning of the Order of Friars Minor (*Ordo Fratrum Minorum*).

The brotherhood grew rapidly across Europe. Francis was joined by his spiritual partner <SaintLink slug="saint-clare-of-assisi">Saint Clare of Assisi</SaintLink>, who founded the Order of Poor Ladies (the Poor Clares) under his guidance. Francis insisted on absolute poverty — his friars were to own nothing individually or communally, living entirely by alms and the work of their hands.

## Mission and the Sultan

In 1219, driven by a desire for martyrdom and dialogue, Francis sailed to Egypt during the Fifth Crusade. He crossed enemy lines and met Sultan Malik al-Kamil, engaging in a peaceful theological exchange. The Sultan, impressed by Francis's sincerity, allowed him to return safely — a remarkable episode of interfaith encounter in the midst of war.

## The Stigmata

In September 1224, while fasting on the mountain of La Verna in Tuscany, Francis experienced a powerful vision of a seraph — a six-winged angelic being — appearing in the form of Christ crucified. When the vision faded, Francis discovered wounds in his hands, feet, and side corresponding to those of the crucified Christ. He bore these stigmata until his death, keeping them largely hidden and approaching them with both wonder and suffering.

## Death and Legacy

By 1226, Francis's health had severely deteriorated. Nearly blind, he composed his famous *Canticle of the Sun* (*Canticle of Brother Sun*), one of the earliest works of Italian literature, praising God through all of creation. He died on the evening of October 3, 1226, near the Portiuncula chapel in Assisi, singing Psalm 141.

He was canonized just two years later in 1228 by Pope Gregory IX, an extraordinarily swift canonization reflecting his universal impact. In 1979, Pope John Paul II declared Francis the patron saint of ecology. Today, the Franciscan family — including the Friars Minor, Capuchins, Conventuals, and Third Order — numbers in the hundreds of thousands worldwide.
