## Overview

Saint Matthias was the Apostle chosen by the early Church to replace Judas Iscariot and restore the number of the Apostles to twelve following Christ's Ascension. Having followed Jesus from His baptism by John until His Ascension, Matthias was a dedicated witness to the Resurrection. 

According to ancient tradition, he preached the Gospel in Judea, Cappadocia, and Colchis (modern-day Georgia) and ultimately suffered martyrdom. He is venerated as the patron saint of carpenters, tailors, and those struggling with addiction.

## Life before the Apostolate

Matthias was not one of the original Twelve Apostles chosen during Jesus' earthly ministry. However, he was a devoted disciple from the very beginning. 

He was part of the larger group of disciples who accompanied Jesus throughout His public ministry, witnessing His teachings, miracles, and ultimately His Resurrection and Ascension. This long-standing faithfulness made him uniquely qualified for the apostolic ministry.

## Selection to the Twelve

Following the Ascension of Jesus and before the descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, Peter addressed the gathered community of 120 believers in Jerusalem. He explained the necessity of choosing a replacement for Judas Iscariot to restore the symbolic number of twelve. 

The criteria was that the candidate must have been with them during Jesus' entire ministry and a witness to His Resurrection. Two men were proposed: Joseph called Barsabbas, and Matthias. After praying for guidance, the community cast lots. The lot fell to Matthias, and he was added to the Eleven Apostles.

## Mission and Travel

Like the other Apostles, Matthias set out to preach the Gospel after Pentecost. Christian traditions regarding his journeys vary. 

Some early accounts state that he preached in Judea, while others record him traveling to Cappadocia (modern Turkey), the Caspian Sea regions, and Colchis (modern Georgia), where he established early Christian communities among pagan populations. Some traditions also suggest he traveled as far as Ethiopia.

## Martyrdom

Various accounts describe Matthias's martyrdom. The most widely accepted tradition in the Western Church is that he was stoned in Jerusalem by pagan crowds for his fearless preaching of Christ and was subsequently beheaded with an axe. 

Other eastern traditions record that he was crucified in Colchis. The Catholic Church honors him as a martyr who remained faithful unto death.

## Relics and Legacy

Matthias's relics have a unique history. Empress Saint Helena traditionally brought his relics from Jerusalem to Rome. Later, a portion of these relics was sent to Trier, Germany, where they are now housed at St. Matthias Abbey. This is the only tomb of an Apostle located north of the Alps. 

Other relics of Saint Matthias are venerated in the Basilica of Santa Maria Maggiore in Rome. He is invoked as the patron of tailors, carpenters, and those seeking hope and perseverance in faith.
