## Overview

Saint Thérèse of Lisieux — known as *La Petite Thérèse* or the "Little Flower" — was a French Carmelite nun who died of tuberculosis at age 24, having never left her convent or performed any publicly notable deeds. Yet her posthumously published autobiography, *The Story of a Soul*, became one of the most widely read spiritual books in history. Pope Pius X called her "the greatest saint of modern times." In 1997, Pope John Paul II declared her a Doctor of the Church — at age 24 at death, one of the youngest ever to receive this title.

## A Childhood Marked by Love and Loss

Thérèse Martin was born on January 2, 1873, in Alençon, the youngest of nine children of Louis and Zélie Martin (both canonized as a married couple in 2015). Four of her nine siblings died in infancy. Her mother Zélie died of breast cancer when Thérèse was only four years old, an event that shattered her emotionally and began a period she described as her "second period" of intense sensitivity and scrupulosity.

The family moved to Lisieux, where Thérèse was raised in an atmosphere of fervent Catholic piety. Her oldest sister Pauline entered the Carmelite convent there — a loss Thérèse experienced as a "second mother" disappearing. She herself felt powerfully drawn to Carmel from an early age.

## The Christmas Conversion

On Christmas Eve 1886, the fourteen-year-old Thérèse experienced what she called her "complete conversion" — she overheard her father making a tired remark about her childishness over Christmas shoes, and instead of dissolving in tears as had been her custom, she felt a sudden interior calm and joy. She later wrote that God restored in a moment the strength of soul she had lost at four.

## The Audience with Pope Leo XIII

Determined to enter Carmel despite being considered too young (the minimum age was 21), Thérèse accompanied her father and sister on a diocesan pilgrimage to Rome in 1887. During a papal audience, she broke protocol to kneel before Pope Leo XIII and ask directly for permission to enter Carmel at fifteen. The Pope replied: *"You will enter if God wills it."* Permission was eventually granted, and Thérèse entered the Carmel of Lisieux on April 9, 1888, at age fifteen.

## The Little Way

Thérèse's great spiritual contribution was her discovery and articulation of the "Little Way" — a path to holiness not through great deeds, extraordinary penances, or mystical experiences, but through radical trust in God's merciful love and the faithful, loving performance of ordinary daily tasks.

She compared herself to a little child who cannot climb the steep staircase of spiritual perfection by her own effort but asks to be taken up by God as in an elevator. Her insight was revolutionary: holiness is accessible to everyone, not just spiritual athletes.

## The Dark Night and Death

In her final eighteen months, Thérèse suffered a severe spiritual trial — she could feel no consolation, and the very existence of heaven seemed like a fairy tale. She carried this darkness with charity and trust, offering it for the sake of souls who had no faith. Meanwhile, tuberculosis was consuming her.

She died on September 30, 1897, reportedly saying: *"My God, I love you!"* She was 24 years old. Her last written words were: *"I do not regret having surrendered myself to love."*

## The Shower of Roses

Before her death, Thérèse promised: *"After my death I will let fall a shower of roses."* Reports of miraculous healings and answered prayers flooded in after her death. Her rose became the symbol of her intercession, linking her to her patron, the Carmelite mystic <SaintLink slug="teresa-of-avila">Saint Teresa of Ávila</SaintLink>.

## The Story of a Soul

*Histoire d'une Âme* (*The Story of a Soul*), compiled by her sister Pauline from manuscripts after Thérèse's death, was a publishing sensation. Translated into dozens of languages, it brought Thérèse's message to every continent. Her canonization in 1925 — a mere 28 years after her death — was attended by 500,000 people.
