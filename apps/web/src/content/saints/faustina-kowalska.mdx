## Overview

Saint Maria Faustina Kowalska, globally known as the Apostle of Divine Mercy, was a Polish nun and mystic whose mystical experiences and visions of Jesus Christ inspired the Catholic devotion to the Divine Mercy. Canonized in 2000 by Pope John Paul II, she is remembered for her spiritual diary, *Divine Mercy in My Soul*, which has become a classic of Catholic mysticism and a source of comfort to millions around the world.

## Early Life and Calling

Born Helena Kowalska on August 25, 1905, in Głogowiec, Poland, she was the third of ten children in a poor and religious peasant family. Her calling to the religious life was evident early on; she reportedly first heard the voice of Jesus calling her when she was only seven years old. Despite her strong desire to enter a convent, her parents repeatedly refused her permission due to their financial inability to provide the required dowry.

After experiencing a vision of the suffering Jesus at a dance in Łódź, Helena immediately travelled to Warsaw. Following several rejections by various religious orders, she was finally accepted by the Congregation of the Sisters of Our Lady of Mercy in 1925, taking the name Sister Maria Faustina. 

## The Divine Mercy Revelations

Sister Faustina worked as a cook, gardener, and porter in various houses of her congregation, most notably in Warsaw, Vilnius, and Kraków. Despite her seemingly ordinary life, she experienced a profound mystical union with God. 

On February 22, 1931, while in her cell in Płock, Jesus appeared to her in a vision that would change the course of Catholic history. He was dressed in a white garment, with one hand raised in blessing and the other touching the garment at the breast. From the breast issued two large rays—one red and the other pale. Jesus asked her to have an image painted according to this pattern, with the signature: *"Jesus, I trust in You."* 

This became the famous Image of Divine Mercy, symbolizing the blood and water that flowed from Jesus' pierced heart on the cross, representing the Eucharist and Baptism (or the Holy Spirit). 

## Diary and The Chaplet

Under obedience to her spiritual directors, particularly Blessed Michael Sopoćko, Faustina meticulously recorded her visions and conversations with Jesus in her diary. The diary reveals a soul intimately united with Christ and deeply concerned for the salvation of souls, especially sinners.

Through her visions, Jesus requested the establishment of the Feast of Divine Mercy on the Sunday after Easter and taught her the Chaplet of Divine Mercy, a prayer intended to appease the wrath of God and obtain mercy for the world. He promised extraordinary graces to those who venerate the image and pray the chaplet with trust.

## Illness and Death

Sister Faustina suffered immensely from tuberculosis, which affected her lungs and gastrointestinal tract. She bore her physical sufferings with heroic patience, offering them in union with Christ's passion for the conversion of sinners. She spent the last years of her life in the convent in Kraków-Łagiewniki.

She died on October 5, 1938, at the age of 33, exactly as she had predicted. Her mission, however, was just beginning. 

## Legacy and Canonization

Although the Divine Mercy devotion faced initial skepticism and was temporarily banned by the Vatican, it was eventually championed by Archbishop Karol Wojtyła of Kraków, who later became Pope John Paul II.

On April 30, 2000—the first Sunday after Easter, which he officially designated as Divine Mercy Sunday—Pope John Paul II canonized Saint Faustina, calling her "the great apostle of Divine Mercy in our time." Her diary remains one of the most widely read spiritual books, and the Divine Mercy devotion has become a central part of contemporary Catholic piety.
