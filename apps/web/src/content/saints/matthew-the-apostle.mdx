## Overview

Saint Matthew the Apostle, also known as Levi, was a Jewish tax collector whom Jesus personally called to leave everything and follow Him. His conversion is a powerful testimony to God's mercy, demonstrating that no one is beyond Christ's invitation to discipleship. 

Matthew became one of the Twelve Apostles and, according to Catholic tradition, authored the first Gospel, presenting Jesus as the long-awaited Messiah. After Pentecost, he preached the Gospel in various regions and is traditionally honored as a martyr.

## Life before Following Jesus

Before his conversion, Matthew worked as a tax collector (publican) for the Roman authorities in Capernaum. Tax collectors were deeply despised by the Jewish population because they collaborated with the Roman occupiers and frequently engaged in extortion and dishonesty to enrich themselves. Despite his controversial and unpopular profession, Jesus saw Matthew's heart and chose him to join His inner circle of disciples.

## Call to Discipleship

Matthew's call was sudden and powerful. While sitting at his tax booth, Jesus simply looked at him and said: *"Follow me."* Matthew immediately left his lucrative profession, his wealth, and his old life behind to follow Christ. 

Later, Matthew hosted a grand banquet at his home for Jesus, inviting many tax collectors and sinners. When Pharisees questioned why Jesus ate with such outcasts, Jesus replied: *"It is not the healthy who need a physician, but the sick. I desire mercy, not sacrifice."*

## The Gospel of Matthew

Christian tradition attributes the authorship of the First Gospel to Saint Matthew. Written primarily for Jewish-Christian audiences, his Gospel focuses heavily on proving that Jesus is the Messiah and the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies. 

Matthew's Gospel is unique in containing the visit of the Magi, the Sermon on the Mount, and the Great Commission: *"Go therefore and make disciples of all nations..."* In Christian art, Matthew is symbolized by the winged man (or angel) because his Gospel opens with the human genealogy of Jesus.

## Mission and Martyrdom

Following the events of Pentecost, Matthew preached the Good News throughout Judea. Ancient traditions record that he later traveled to other lands to mission, including Ethiopia, Persia, Syria, and Parthia. 

While some early accounts suggest he died a natural death, most historical Christian traditions hold that he was martyred for his faith—frequently depicted as being slain with a sword or halberd while celebrating the Liturgy.

## Relics and Legacy

Saint Matthew is highly venerated as the patron saint of accountants, bankers, tax collectors, financial workers, and civil servants. His relics were translated in the 10th century to Italy and are today housed in the crypt of the Cathedral of Salerno (Duomo di Salerno), which remains a major pilgrimage site.
