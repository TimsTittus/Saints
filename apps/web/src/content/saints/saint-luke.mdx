## Overview

Saint Luke the Evangelist was a first-century physician, missionary companion of Saint Paul, and the author of both the Gospel of Luke and the Acts of the Apostles. His two-volume work makes up a substantial portion of the New Testament. 

Traditionally regarded as a Greek Gentile from Antioch, Luke wrote with a refined historical methodology and a deep focus on Christ's compassion, mercy, and universal salvation. He is symbolized by the winged ox.

## Life and Relationship with Saint Paul

Luke was highly educated and practiced medicine. Saint Paul affectionately referred to him in scripture as *"Luke the beloved physician"* (Colossians 4:14). Luke became one of Paul's closest and most loyal companions, traveling with him on missionary journeys and remaining with him during his imprisonments in Rome. 

Paul wrote during his final days, *"Only Luke is with me"* (2 Timothy 4:11). The "we" passages in the Acts of the Apostles indicate Luke's firsthand participation in many of Paul's travels.

## Author of the Gospel and Acts

Luke's Gospel is famous for its emphasis on the universal call to salvation, mercy, joy, the Holy Spirit, and prayer. He provides the most detailed account of the Annunciation, the Visitation, and Jesus' infancy, likely gathering eyewitness accounts directly from the Blessed Virgin Mary. 

His Gospel contains beloved parables found nowhere else, such as the Prodigal Son and the Good Samaritan. As a continuation of his Gospel, Luke wrote the Acts of the Apostles, providing an invaluable historical record of Pentecost and the expansion of the early Church from Jerusalem to Rome.

## Later Life and Death

Following Saint Paul's martyrdom, ancient tradition states that Luke continued preaching the Gospel across Greece, Dalmatia, and Macedonia. 

He lived a celibate life devoted to the Lord and died at an advanced age (around 84) in Boeotia, Greece. While some traditions suggest he died peacefully, others record that he was martyred by hanging from an olive tree.

## Relics, Symbols, and Legacy

Saint Luke is symbolized by a **winged ox**, representing sacrifice, strength, and priesthood, as his Gospel begins with the priest Zechariah offering sacrifice in the Temple. 

According to ancient tradition, Luke was also an iconographer and is said to have painted the first icon of the Blessed Virgin Mary, making him the patron saint of artists and painters, in addition to physicians, surgeons, and historians. His primary relics are venerated in the Basilica of Saint Justina in Padua, Italy.
