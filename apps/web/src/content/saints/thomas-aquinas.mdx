## Overview

Saint Thomas Aquinas, known as the *Doctor Angelicus* (Angelic Doctor), stands as the pre-eminent theologian of the Catholic Church. His synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy with Christian faith — articulated above all in his unfinished *Summa Theologica* — profoundly shaped the intellectual foundations of Western civilization. Pope Leo XIII's encyclical *Aeterni Patris* (1879) recommended Thomism as the basis of Catholic philosophical and theological education, a status it retains today.

## Early Life and Education

Thomas was born around 1225 into the noble Aquino family near Naples. Destined for an ecclesiastical career, he was oblated at age five to the Benedictine Abbey of Monte Cassino. After studying at the University of Naples, he encountered the recently founded Order of Preachers (Dominicans) and determined to join them — a decision his aristocratic family found scandalous.

His brothers actually kidnapped him and held him captive for over a year, reportedly even sending a courtesan to tempt him. Thomas reportedly drove her away with a burning brand from the fire. When finally released, he joined the Dominicans and was sent to Cologne to study under the great philosopher-theologian <SaintLink slug="albert-the-great">Albert the Great</SaintLink>.

## Academic Career

Thomas's fellow students initially nicknamed him "the dumb ox" for his large, silent demeanor. Albert reportedly predicted: *"You call him a dumb ox, but I tell you this dumb ox shall bellow so loud that his bellowings will fill the world."* Thomas went on to study and teach in Paris, Cologne, Rome, Naples, and Viterbo.

His Parisian career was marked by controversy — the mendicant orders faced fierce opposition from secular masters at the University of Paris, and Aquinas wrote several works defending their right to teach. He obtained the chair of theology at Paris in 1256.

## The Summa Theologica

Thomas's masterwork is the *Summa Theologica* (*Summary of Theology*), begun around 1265 and left unfinished at his death. Its scope is breathtaking: over 3,000 articles organized into three parts, addressing the nature of God, creation, morality, the Incarnation, and the Sacraments. Each article follows a strict *quaestio* format: objections are stated, a definitive answer given, and objections replied to systematically.

The Summa became the standard textbook of Catholic theology and philosophy. At the Council of Trent (1545–1563), it was placed on the altar alongside the Bible and the Papal Decretals.

## Mysticism and the Unfinished Work

On December 6, 1273, while celebrating Mass in Naples, Thomas experienced a profound mystical vision. He never wrote again. When his secretary urged him to resume, he reportedly said: *"I cannot, because all that I have written seems like straw to me."* He died on March 7, 1274, en route to the Second Council of Lyon.

## Philosophical Contributions

Thomas's theology rests on the principle that faith and reason are complementary, not contradictory. His five philosophical proofs (*quinque viae*) for the existence of God — including the argument from motion, causation, contingency, degrees of perfection, and teleology — remain foundational in natural theology.
