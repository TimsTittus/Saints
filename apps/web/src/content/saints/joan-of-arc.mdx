## Overview

Saint Joan of Arc — known in France as *Jeanne d'Arc* and *La Pucelle d'Orléans* (The Maid of Orléans) — is one of the most remarkable figures in world history. A teenage peasant girl from a remote French village, she claimed to have received divine visions commanding her to support Charles VII and expel the English from France. Within a year, she had reversed the tide of the Hundred Years' War. Captured, tried for heresy, and burned at the stake at age 19, she was posthumously exonerated and eventually canonized in 1920. She remains France's national heroine and patron saint.

## Early Life in Domrémy

Joan was born around January 6, 1412, in the village of Domrémy (now Domrémy-la-Pucelle) in the Duchy of Bar, on the borders of Champagne and Lorraine. Her father, Jacques d'Arc, was a peasant farmer. Joan could not read or write but reportedly excelled in piety and needlework.

By her own account, Joan began hearing voices at age thirteen — which she identified as Saints Michael, Catherine, and Margaret — urging her to support the Dauphin Charles VII and recover France from English and Burgundian control during the long dynastic conflict of the Hundred Years' War.

## The Mission Begins

In 1428, Joan attempted to make contact with Robert de Baudricourt, the commander of a nearby garrison loyal to Charles VII, seeking an escort to the royal court. Twice rebuffed, she persisted. In February 1429, Baudricourt relented and provided her with an escort of soldiers. Joan traveled in disguise through enemy-controlled territory — a journey of eleven days.

Arriving at Chinon, she reportedly identified the Dauphin at court despite his attempt to disguise himself among his nobles. She told him she had been sent by God to deliver France. After examinations by theologians to verify her orthodoxy, she was outfitted with armor, given a sword, and placed at the head of a relief army.

## The Relief of Orléans

In April 1429, Joan led the French army to the besieged city of Orléans, which had been under English siege for months. Within nine days of her arrival, the English had abandoned all their siege fortifications — an astonishing reversal. The liberation of Orléans (May 8, 1429) became Joan's greatest military triumph and a turning point in the Hundred Years' War.

She subsequently led French forces to several additional victories in the Loire campaign, clearing the way for the Dauphin's coronation.

## The Coronation of Charles VII

Joan's primary spiritual mission was to see Charles VII crowned King of France at Reims Cathedral, the traditional coronation site. Against significant military and political odds, she achieved this goal on July 17, 1429. Standing by the King's side during the ceremony, Joan later said this was the fulfillment of her mission.

## Capture and Trial

In May 1430, Joan was captured by the Burgundians near Compiègne and sold to the English. She was tried before a church court in Rouen, presided over by Bishop Pierre Cauchon — a partisan of the English and Burgundian factions. The trial was politically motivated: discrediting Joan would discredit her king's coronation.

The charges included heresy and cross-dressing (wearing men's clothing). After a long ordeal of interrogation, she was threatened with burning and briefly abjured — before recanting her abjuration and resuming men's clothing, which sealed her fate.

## Martyrdom and Rehabilitation

Joan was burned at the stake on May 30, 1431, in the Old Market Square of Rouen. She asked for a cross to be held before her and reportedly called on Jesus until the end. The English official Henry Beaufort reportedly said: *"We are lost; we have burned a saint."*

Twenty-five years later, a Rehabilitation Trial (1455–1456) commissioned by Pope Callixtus III found her innocent, annulling the condemnation. She was beatified in 1909 and canonized in 1920.

## Legacy

Joan of Arc is one of the most depicted figures in Western art and literature, from Shakespeare and Schiller to Mark Twain and George Bernard Shaw. She has been claimed as a symbol by feminists, nationalists, Catholics, and romantics alike. In France, May 30 is celebrated as her national feast day.
