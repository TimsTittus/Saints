## Overview

Saint James the Greater, also known as James, Son of Zebedee, was one of the Twelve Apostles chosen by Jesus Christ. Alongside his brother Saint John and Saint Peter, James belonged to Jesus' closest inner circle, witnessing key events such as the Transfiguration and the Agony in Gethsemane. 

He was nicknamed a "Son of Thunder" (Boanerges) by Jesus, reflecting his passionate zeal. Around AD 44, he became the first of the Apostles to be martyred, executed by the sword in Jerusalem. According to ancient Christian tradition, his relics reside in Santiago de Compostela, making him the patron saint of Spain and pilgrims.

## Early Life and Calling

James was born in the early 1st century AD, likely in Bethsaida, Galilee. He worked as a fisherman with his father Zebedee and his brother John. Their lives changed forever when Jesus approached them while they were mending nets on the shores of the Sea of Galilee. 

Jesus called them immediately, and without hesitation, the brothers left their father, their boat, and their family business behind to follow Him. This prompt response serves as a classic biblical example of instant obedience and faith.

## Why "the Greater"?

To distinguish him from another Apostle named James (James, Son of Alphaeus, or James the Less), the Church refers to him as "James the Greater." This title does not imply that he was holier or held higher spiritual standing. Instead, it likely indicates that he was older in age, called earlier in Jesus' ministry, or held a more prominent role among the disciples.

## The Inner Circle and Sons of Thunder

James and John possessed fiery, passionate personalities, leading Jesus to nickname them *Boanerges*, meaning "Sons of Thunder." This passionate nature was evident when they suggested calling down fire from heaven to consume a Samaritan village that had rejected Jesus, earning a gentle rebuke from their Master.

Despite their shortcomings, James was one of only three Apostles (along with Peter and John) who formed Jesus' intimate inner circle. They alone were permitted to witness three major events: the raising of Jairus' daughter, the glorious Transfiguration on the mount, and Jesus' agonized prayer in the Garden of Gethsemane on the night of His arrest.

## Mission after Pentecost

According to longstanding Christian tradition, following Pentecost, Saint James traveled beyond Judea and Samaria to preach the Gospel in Spain (the Iberian Peninsula). While the New Testament does not record this journey, the tradition of his Spanish mission has been deeply cherished for over a millennium. 

According to these accounts, James preached the faith to the Celtic peoples of Spain, though he met with limited initial success. He eventually returned to Jerusalem, where his mission would come to a sudden end.

## Martyrdom

Around AD 44, during a period of intense persecution of the early Church, King Herod Agrippa I targeted the leaders of the Christian community. James was arrested and subsequently executed by the sword in Jerusalem, as recorded in Acts 12:1-2. He became the first of the Twelve Apostles to suffer martyrdom for his faith, and he remains the only Apostle whose death is explicitly recorded in the New Testament.

## Legacy and The Camino de Santiago

Following his martyrdom, tradition states that his disciples carried his remains back to Spain by sea. His tomb was rediscovered in the 9th century in Galicia, leading to the construction of the Cathedral of Santiago de Compostela. 

This shrine became the focal point of the *Camino de Santiago* ("Way of Saint James"), one of the most famous Christian pilgrimage routes in the world. For over a thousand years, millions of pilgrims have walked this path, carrying the scallop shell—the traditional symbol of the pilgrimage and of Saint James.
