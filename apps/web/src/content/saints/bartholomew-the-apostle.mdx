## Overview

Saint Bartholomew, traditionally identified as Nathanael, was one of the Twelve Apostles chosen by Jesus Christ. In the Gospels, he is portrayed as a man of great sincerity and honesty, whom Jesus praised as *"an Israelite indeed, in whom there is no deceit."* 

According to ancient Christian tradition, after the events of Pentecost, Bartholomew traveled extensively to preach the Gospel across several countries, most notably Armenia, where he was martyred. He is revered globally as the patron saint of bookbinders, leather workers, and tanners.

## Call to Discipleship

Bartholomew is traditionally identified in the Gospel of John as Nathanael. His call to discipleship occurred through his friend Philip, who excitedly told him, *"We have found him of whom Moses in the Law and also the prophets wrote, Jesus of Nazareth, the son of Joseph."* Nathanael initially reacted with skepticism, famously asking: *"Can anything good come out of Nazareth?"* Philip simply replied, *"Come and see."*

When Jesus saw Nathanael approaching, He revealed His divine nature by telling him He saw him under the fig tree before Philip called him. Stunned by this supernatural knowledge, Nathanael immediately declared: *"Rabbi, you are the Son of God! You are the King of Israel!"* This was one of the earliest recorded confessions of faith in Christ.

## Role among the Twelve

Bartholomew is listed as one of the Twelve Apostles in all three Synoptic Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke) as well as the Acts of the Apostles. In these lists, he is almost always paired with Philip, corroborating the account of their friendship in John's Gospel where Philip introduced him to Jesus. While John's Gospel refers to him as Nathanael, the other Gospels use his patronymic name "Bartholomew," which translates from Aramaic as "Son of Tolmai."

## Mission after Pentecost

Following the Descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, Bartholomew set out on extensive missionary journeys to spread the Christian faith. According to historical traditions recorded by early Church writers, he preached in various regions, including Mesopotamia, Persia, Arabia, parts of India, and Ethiopia. His most significant work was in Armenia, where he, alongside Saint Jude Thaddeus, successfully converted many people to Christianity. Because of their pioneering missionary efforts, both Jude and Bartholomew are venerated as the patron saints of the Armenian Apostolic Church.

## Martyrdom

Saint Bartholomew's missionary success in Armenia eventually led to his arrest. The local pagan priests grew furious as he converted the Armenian King Polymius to Christianity. King Polymius's brother, Prince Astyages, ordered Bartholomew to be tortured and executed in Albanopolis. 

According to longstanding tradition, Bartholomew was flayed alive (his skin was peeled off) and subsequently beheaded. He bore his extreme suffering with heroic courage, refusing to renounce his faith in Jesus Christ, which made him one of the most honored martyrs in Christian history.

## Legacy and Relics

Bartholomew's legacy remains strong within Christian iconography. Because of the nature of his martyrdom, he is often depicted in art holding a flaying knife or carrying his own skin (as seen in Michelangelo's *Last Judgment* in the Sistine Chapel). 

His relics were eventually moved to Rome in the late 10th century by Emperor Otto III and are now housed in the Basilica of St. Bartholomew on the Island (Tiber Island). Other parts of his relics are venerated in Benevento, Italy, and Frankfurt Cathedral in Germany.
