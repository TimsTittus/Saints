## Overview

Saint Andrew, also known as Saint Andrew the First-Called (or Protoclete), was one of the Twelve Apostles of Jesus Christ and the older brother of Saint Peter. Originally a disciple of John the Baptist, Andrew was the very first Apostle to follow Jesus, immediately introducing Peter to Christ. 

He preached the Gospel in the regions surrounding the Black Sea, Asia Minor, and Greece. According to ancient Christian tradition, Andrew was martyred in Patras, Greece, by being bound to an X-shaped cross.

## Early Life and Discipleship

Andrew was born in the early 1st century AD in Bethsaida on the Sea of Galilee. He worked as a fisherman in partnership with his brother Simon Peter. Andrew was a spiritual seeker and initially became a disciple of John the Baptist. 

One day, John the Baptist pointed toward Jesus and declared: *"Behold, the Lamb of God!"* Hearing this, Andrew immediately left John to follow Jesus. He then eagerly found his brother Simon Peter and announced: *"We have found the Messiah!"*—before bringing Peter to meet Christ.

## Life with Jesus

Andrew is mentioned in several key moments in the Gospels. At the Feeding of the Five Thousand, it was Andrew who brought the young boy with five barley loaves and two fish to Jesus' attention, saying: *"There is a boy here who has five barley loaves and two fish, but what are they for so many?"* 

Andrew was also present alongside Philip when a group of Greeks sought to meet Jesus, showing his role as an intermediary and a welcoming disciple who regularly brought others to the Lord.

## Missionary Journeys

Following the Descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, Andrew undertook extensive missionary journeys. According to the early Church historian Eusebius and local traditions, Andrew traveled around the Black Sea, Scythia (modern-day Russia and Ukraine), Thrace (modern-day Bulgaria and Turkey), and Asia Minor. 

He is traditionally regarded as the founder and first Bishop of the Church of Byzantium (later Constantinople), mirroring his brother Peter's role in Rome.

## Martyrdom

Andrew eventually arrived in Patras, in the region of Achaea (modern-day Greece), where his preaching converted many, including the wife and brother of the Roman governor Aegeas. Furious at the conversions, Aegeas ordered Andrew to be arrested and crucified. 

Out of deep humility and reverence for Christ, Andrew requested not to be crucified on the same type of cross as Jesus. He was therefore bound (rather than nailed) to an X-shaped cross (crux decussata). He survived for three days, preaching to the crowds from the cross before dying.

## Legacy and Relics

Saint Andrew's primary symbol is the X-shaped cross, which is featured on several national flags, most notably the Saltire (flag of Scotland). His relics are venerated in the Cathedral of Saint Andrew in Patras, Greece, and the Amalfi Cathedral in Italy. 

He is honored as the patron saint of Scotland, Greece, Russia, Romania, Ukraine, and fishermen.
